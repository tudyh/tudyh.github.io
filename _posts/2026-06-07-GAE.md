## Introduction

In Reinforcement Learning, policy-based methods maximize the expected reward by computing its gradient with respect to the policy parameters.  

$$formule poicy gradient$$

Several choices exist for $\Psi_t$, but we need to keep in mind that we will need to estimate this gradient. The choice of the advantage function yields one of the lowest variance possible. Yet, as we don't know $V^{\pi}$ and $Q^{\pi}$, we need to construct an estimator for this advantage function. 


A key source of difficulty in Reinforcement Learning is the long time interval between actions and their effect on reward. Given some policy $\pi$, the Value function and the Action-value function offer an elegant solution to this problem, allowing us to estimate the "goodness" of a state and/or an action several steps before the agent receives a reward. 

Given these two functions, another quantity of interest arises: the advantage function $A^{\pi}(s,a) = Q^{\pi}(s,a) - V^{\pi}(s)$. This function possesses a simple interpretation: it evaluates whether an action yields better results than the average policy behavior in a given state. Intuitively, it offers a very strong signal about how to optimize our policy: we should prioritize actions giving better-than-average return. Yet, in practice, neither $Q^{\pi}(s,a)$ nor $V^{\pi}(s)$ is known, and thus we need to estimate $A^{\pi}$. This blog post aims at presenting the method used by Schulman et al. in their article HIGH-DIMENSIONAL CONTINUOUS CONTROL USING
GENERALIZED ADVANTAGE ESTIMATION, while giving some insight about how to use it in practice. 

## How to estimate the Advantage function?

First, let's do a quick overview of our problem setting. We assume that we can simulate trajectories with our agent. Thus, a sensible way to approximate the Q-function at timestep t $Q^{\pi}(s_t,a_t)$ will be to use the discounted return obtained from this step onwards, $\sum_{l \geq 0} \gamma^l r_{t+l}$. Indeed, since the action $a_t$ is the one actually taken in the trajectory, this return is an unbiased sample of $Q^{\pi}(s_t,a_t)$. 

Why, then, do we also introduce an approximate value function $V$ rather than estimating everything from returns? There are two structural reasons. First, the advantage $A^{\pi}(s_t,a_t) = Q^{\pi}(s_t,a_t) - V^{\pi}(s_t)$ needs a baseline $V^{\pi}(s_t)$, which we do not get for free from a single return. Second, as we will see, bootstrapping with $V$ is precisely what will let us trade bias for variance in our estimates.

Using these tools, let's define the TD-residual at timestep t : $\delta^V_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$. We should note that if our estimate of the value function was exact, i.e., $V = V^\pi$, $\delta^V_t$ would define an unbiased estimate of the advantage. 

Indeed : 

$$Q^{\pi}(s_t, a_t) = \mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[r_t + \gamma V^{\pi}(s_{t+1})\right]$$

where the expectation is taken over the reward $r_t$ and the next state $s_{t+1}$ drawn from the environment given $(s_t, a_t)$. Hence, conditioning on $(s_t, a_t)$:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[\delta_t^{V^\pi}\right]
&= \mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[r_t + \gamma V^{\pi}(s_{t+1}) - V^{\pi}(s_t)\right] \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[r_t + \gamma V^{\pi}(s_{t+1})\right] - V^{\pi}(s_t) \\
&= Q^{\pi}(s_t, a_t) - V^{\pi}(s_t) = A^{\pi}(s_t, a_t).
\end{aligned}
\]


In this ideal situation, we would be equipped with an unbiased low-variance estimator of $A^{\pi}(s_t,a_t)$. Unfortunately, we often have some bias in our estimate $V$ of $V^\pi$. Yet, what would happen if we "looked more steps into the future" to estimate our advantage? Let's define:  

$$\hat{A}_t^{(k)} := r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \cdots + \gamma^{k-1} r_{t+k-1} + \gamma^k V(s_{t+k}) -V(s_t)$$

For all $k$, $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$ is an estimate of the advantage whose reliance on $V$ to estimate the return decreases with $k$ as $\gamma^k V(s_{t+k}) \rightarrow 0$. The bigger k is, the less-biased our estimate of the return is, at the cost of an increased variance. 

To see why, it helps to split $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$ into a sampled part and a bootstrapped part :

$$\hat{A}_t^{(k)} = \underbrace{\Big(r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \cdots + \gamma^{k-1}r_{t+k-1}\Big)}_{\text{sum of } k \text{ sampled rewards}} \; + \; \underbrace{\gamma^k V(s_{t+k})}_{\text{bootstrap}} \; - \; V(s_t)$$

Each sampled reward we add to the first term injects a fresh piece of randomness coming from the environment's transition and reward distributions, so the variance of this part tends to grow as $k$ increases. The bootstrap term, on the other hand, replaces an entire random tail of future rewards with an expectation of that tail $V(s_{t+k})$ which has much lower variance, but it is only correct if $V = V^\pi$, which is exactly where the bias comes from. 

Let's note that, using a telescoping sum (for instance $\hat{A}_t^{(2)} = (r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)) + \gamma (r_{t+1} + \gamma V(s_{t+2}) - V(s_{t+1}))$), this estimator can be rewritten as: 

$$\hat{A}_t^{(k)} =  \sum_{l=0}^{k-1} \gamma^l \delta_{t+l}^V$$

This will be useful to obtain a practical expression of our final estimator later. 

Yet one question remains, how to select $k$? This question is difficult to answer, and rather than making a choice, the authors of the paper propose to use an exponentially-weighted average of these estimators, introducing a parameter $\lambda \in (0,1)$<sup>[1]</sup>. 

\[
\begin{aligned}
\hat{A}_t^{\text{GAE}(\gamma,\lambda)} 
&:= (1-\lambda)\left(\hat{A}_t^{(1)} + \lambda\hat{A}_t^{(2)} + \lambda^2\hat{A}_t^{(3)} + \ldots\right) \\
&= (1-\lambda)(\delta_t^V + \lambda(\delta_t^V + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V) + \lambda^2(\delta_t^V + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V + \gamma^2\delta_{t+2}^V) + \ldots) \\
&= (1-\lambda)(\delta_t^V(1 + \lambda + \lambda^2 + \ldots) + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V(\lambda + \lambda^2 + \lambda^3 + \ldots) \\
&\quad + \gamma^2\delta_{t+2}^V(\lambda^2 + \lambda^3 + \lambda^4 + \ldots) + \ldots) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\left(\delta_t^V\left(\frac{1}{1-\lambda}\right) + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V\left(\frac{\lambda}{1-\lambda}\right) + \gamma^2\delta_{t+2}^V\left(\frac{\lambda^2}{1-\lambda}\right) + \ldots\right) \\
&= \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V
\end{aligned}
\]

This last line is what makes the computation easier in practice. 

To build some intuition about this estimator, let's look at its expression for the two limit values of $\lambda$: 

- In the limit $\lambda \rightarrow 1$, we get that:

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,1)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \gamma^l \delta_{t+l}^V = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \gamma^l r_{t+l} - V(s_t)$$

where the last equality follows from the same telescoping argument as before. 

Thus our estimate of the advantage involves a Monte-Carlo estimate of the return! This reduces bias, but makes it very noisy. 

- On the other hand, in the limit $\lambda \rightarrow 0$, we get that:

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,0)} = \delta_t^V = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$$

Thus, our estimate of the advantage only uses the 1-step TD-error. This reduces variance a lot, but we rely on an inexact value function $V$ which can introduce bias. 

These two limit cases give us intuition about why this exponential averaging of the $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$ family was introduced: it enables us to smoothly interpolate the bias-variance tradeoff by varying $\lambda$. 

## How is GAE implemented in practice ?

### On the PPO Loss

Now that we have constructed our estimator of the Advantage function. Let's see how it can be used in practice. For this, we will focus on one of the most popular policy-based method in RL litterature : PPO. 

Let's recall that the loss at timestep t in PPO is written as :  

$$
L_t(\theta) = L_t^{CLIP}(\theta) - c_1 L_t^{VF}(\theta) + c_2 S[\pi_\theta],
$$

Here, $S[\pi_\theta]$ is an entropy bonus that encourages exploration by discouraging an overly deterministic policy. Since it's not related to our advantage estimate $\hat{A}_t$, we will set it aside and focus on the clipped surrogate objective $L_t^{CLIP}$ and the value-function loss $L_t^{VF}$. 

We have : 

$$
L_t^{CLIP}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\left[\min(r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t)\right]
$$

And : 

$$
L_t^{VF}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\left[(\hat{V_t} - V_{\theta}(s_t))^2\right]
$$

In these two losses, it seems that two estimators are needed, $\hat{A}_t$ and $\hat{V}_t$. To see where $\hat{V}_t$ comes from, it helps to go back to the $k$-step formulation of GAE: recalling that $\hat{A}_t^{(k)} = \sum_{l=0}^{k-1}\gamma^l r_{t+l} + \gamma^k V_{\theta_{old}}(s_{t+k}) - V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t)$ and $\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,\lambda)} = (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\lambda^{k-1}\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$, we can write:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\hat{V}_t := \hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,\lambda)} + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t)
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} \hat{A}_t^{(k)} + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} \Big[\underbrace{\sum_{l=0}^{k-1}\gamma^l r_{t+l} + \gamma^k V_{\theta_{old}}(s_{t+k})}_{=: \, G_t^{(k)}} \;-\; V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t)\Big] + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} G_t^{(k)} \;-\; \underbrace{(1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\lambda^{k-1}}_{=\,1} V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} G_t^{(k)}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Here $G_t^{(k)} = \sum_{l=0}^{k-1}\gamma^l r_{t+l} + \gamma^k V_{\theta_{old}}(s_{t+k})$ is exactly the bootstrapped $k$-step return, so $\hat{V}_t$ is an exponentially-weighted average of these returns, which s precisely the $\lambda$-return used as the regression target in $TD(\lambda)$ ! This equations also reveal where the $+\,V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t)$ term comes from: adding it exactly cancels the baseline $-\,V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t)$ present in every $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$, turning our weighted average of *advantages* back into a weighted average of *returns*. Thus, the GAE computation directly gives us the regression target used in $L_t^{VF}$, making it particularly suitable to the PPO framework. 

That said, we should keep in mind that this identification is more of an inspiration than an exact equivalence. The classical $TD(\lambda)$ target estimates $V^{\pi}$ from trajectories collected under $\pi$ itself, whereas in PPO the rollouts used to build $\hat{A}_t$ -- and hence $\hat{V}_t$ -- are collected under the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$, while $V_\theta$ is being fit to approximate $V^{\pi_\theta}$, the value function of the policy currently being optimized. The target $\hat{V}_t$ should therefore be understood as a practical, off-policy approximation inspired by the $TD(\lambda)$ idea, rather than a mathematically exact instance of it.

### Truncating the sum

Although we've made good progress in our advantage estimation, one problem remains. The formula that we've used for $\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V$ involves an infinite sum, which makes it difficult to compute in practice. However, the original PPO paper propose to truncate the sum at some indice $T$ to make the sum tractable. 

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V$$

In practice, that means that our training batches are constituted of small chunks of trajectories of length $T$. The choice of T varies according to the setup of the problem and system configuration (bigger T often meaning bigger batch size). Yet, intuitively, we need to ensure that the truncated sum carries enough information about the future. The notion of effective horizon helps us doing this choice properly.

### GAE effective horizon

Let's recall that the untruncated advantage estimator is given by the infinite sum : 

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V$$

The discount factor $\gamma\lambda$ causes the reward to shrink geometrically. The key question we need to answer is : after how many steps does the term $(\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V$ becomes negligible ? 

Let's consider the geometric series of weights applied to TD-error : 

$$\sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l = \frac{1}{1- \gamma\lambda}$$

This is the total weight the agent assigns across all future time steps. The effective horizon $H =  \frac{1}{1- \gamma\lambda}$ is the number of steps that capture the "bulk" of this sum. 

Indeed, we can show that after $H$ steps, the weight $\gamma\lambda^H$ has decreased significantly. More precisely : $(\gamma\lambda)^{\frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda}} = e^\frac{ln(\gamma\lambda)}{1-\gamma\lambda} \approx e^{-1}$ if we do a Taylor expansion of $ln$ around $1$. 

What does it mean in practice for our truncation order ? We want to choose $T \geq \frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda}$ to ensure that we do not lose any meaningful information. 

What would happen if we didn't follow this rule ? Let's look at the math : 

\[
\begin{aligned}
\hat{A}_t &= \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V \\
&= \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l r_{t+l} + \gamma \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l V(S_{t+l+1}) - \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l V(S_{t+l}) \\
&= \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l r_{t+l} + \frac{1}{\lambda} \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^{l+1} V(S_{t+l+1}) - \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l V(S_{t+l}) \\
&= \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l r_{t+l} + \left( \frac{1}{\lambda} - 1 \right) \sum_{l=1}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l V(S_{t+l}) + \frac{(\gamma \lambda)^{T+1}}{\lambda} V(S_{t+T+1}) - V(S_t)
\end{aligned}
\]

\[
\begin{aligned}
\hat{A}_t
&= \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l r_{t+l}
   + \left( \frac{1}{\lambda} - 1 \right) \sum_{l=1}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_{t+l})
   + \frac{(\gamma \lambda)^{T+1}}{\lambda} V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_{t+T+1})
   - V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_t) \\[6pt]
\hat{R}_t
&\equiv \hat{A}_t + V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_t) \\
&= \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l r_{t+l}
   + \left( \frac{1}{\lambda} - 1 \right) \sum_{l=1}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_{t+l})
   + \frac{(\gamma \lambda)^{T+1}}{\lambda} V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_{t+T+1}) \\[6pt]
\mathcal{L}^{VF}(\theta_{\text{new}})
&= \tfrac{1}{2}\big( V_{\theta_{\text{new}}}(S_t) - \hat{R}_t \big)^2 \\
&= \tfrac{1}{2}\big( V_{\theta_{\text{new}}}(S_t) - V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_t) - \hat{A}_t \big)^2 \\
&= \tfrac{1}{2}\Bigg( V_{\theta_{\text{new}}}(S_t)
   - \sum_{l=0}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l r_{t+l}
   - \left( \frac{1}{\lambda} - 1 \right) \sum_{l=1}^{T} (\gamma \lambda)^l V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_{t+l})
   - \frac{(\gamma \lambda)^{T+1}}{\lambda} V_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(S_{t+T+1}) \Bigg)^2
\end{aligned}
\]

## Notes

**[1]** This is exactly the same idea as the $\lambda$-return used in $TD(\lambda)$ : instead of picking a single bootstrapping horizon $k$, we average over all of them using an exponential law. 

**[2]** Why does averaging the $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$ estimators actually let us interpolate between their bias and variance, rather than producing some unrelated mixture of behaviors ? The key is that $\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,\lambda)}$ is a convex combination $\sum_{k\geq 1}(1-\lambda)\lambda^{k-1}\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$ of the individual estimators, and both bias and variance respond predictably to such combinations. By linearity of expectation, the bias of this weighted sum is exactly the same weighted average of the biases of the $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$, so it is bounded between the largest bias (achieved at $k=1$, i.e. $\lambda \to 0$, where the estimate leans most heavily on the possibly-inaccurate $V$) and the smallest one (achieved as $k \to \infty$, i.e. $\lambda \to 1$, where the estimate becomes an unbiased Monte-Carlo return); pushing $\lambda$ towards $0$ or $1$ shifts the weight towards one end of this range. Variance behaves similarly in spirit, although less cleanly because the $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$ are correlated (they share most of their terms): increasing $\lambda$ shifts weight towards estimators that rely more on sampled rewards and less on bootstrapping with $V$, which is precisely what drives variance up.
