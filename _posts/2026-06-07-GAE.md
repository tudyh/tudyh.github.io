## Introduction

If you've ever been interested in Reinforcement Learning, there is a high chance you came across Generalized Advantage Estimation [Schulman et al.]<sup><a href="#note-1" id="ref-1">[1]</a></sup> once. Although widely used, this technique is more subtle than it may seem at first glance. This is why I decided to write a short summary of its conceptual ideas and practical nuts and bolts.

## RL setup

First up though, let's do a quick recap about the RL setup.

The goal of Reinforcement Learning is to teach to an agent how to behave through sequential interactions with an environment. Formally, we work in the standard discounted Markov Decision Process (MDP) framework.
At each timestep $t$, the agent observes a state $s_ t \in \mathcal{S}$, samples an action $a_ t \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s_ t)$ from its policy $\pi$, receives a reward $r_ t$, and transitions to a next state $s_ {t+1} \sim P(\cdot \mid s_ t, a_ t)$ according to the environment dynamics. A policy $\pi$ is a (possibly stochastic) mapping from states to distributions over actions, and the agent's goal is to find the policy maximizing the expected discounted return $\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_ {t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t r_ t\right]$, where $\gamma \in (0,1)$ is a discount factor that trades off immediate against future rewards.

A key source of difficulty in Reinforcement Learning is the long time interval between actions and their effect on reward.
Given some policy $\pi$, the Value function and the Action-value function offer an elegant solution to this problem, allowing us to estimate the "goodness" of a state and/or an action several steps before the agent receives a reward.

Formally, given a policy $\pi$, the value function and the action-value (or Q-)function are defined as follows:

$$
V^{\pi}(s) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\!\left[\sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \gamma^l r_{t+l} \,\Big|\, s_t = s\right],
\qquad
Q^{\pi}(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\!\left[\sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \gamma^l r_{t+l} \,\Big|\, s_t = s,\, a_t = a\right],
$$

Given these two functions, another quantity of interest arises: the advantage function $A^{\pi}(s,a) = Q^{\pi}(s,a) - V^{\pi}(s)$. This function possesses a simple interpretation: it evaluates whether an action yields better results than the average policy behavior in a given state. Intuitively, it offers a very strong signal about how to optimize our policy: we should prioritize actions giving better-than-average return. Yet, in practice, neither $Q^{\pi}(s,a)$ nor $V^{\pi}(s)$ is known, and thus we need to estimate $A^{\pi}$.

## How to estimate the Advantage function?

First, let's do a quick overview of our problem setting. We assume that we can simulate trajectories with our agent. Thus, a sensible way to approximate the Q-function at timestep $t$, $Q^{\pi}(s_ t,a_ t)$, will be to use the discounted return obtained from this step onwards, $\sum_ {l \geq 0} \gamma^l r_ {t+l}$. Indeed, since the action $a_ t$ is the one actually taken in the trajectory, this return is an unbiased sample of $Q^{\pi}(s_ t,a_ t)$.

Why, then, do we also introduce an approximate value function $V$ rather than estimating everything from returns? There are two structural reasons. First, the advantage $A^{\pi}(s_ t,a_ t) = Q^{\pi}(s_ t,a_ t) - V^{\pi}(s_ t)$ needs a baseline $V^{\pi}(s_ t)$, which we do not get for free from a single return. Second, as we will see, bootstrapping with $V$ is precisely what will let us trade bias for variance in our estimates.

Using these tools, let's define the TD-residual at timestep $t$: $\delta^V_ t = r_ t + \gamma V(s_ {t+1}) - V(s_ t)$. We should note that if our estimate of the value function was exact, i.e., $V = V^\pi$, $\delta^V_ t$ would define an unbiased estimate of the advantage.

Indeed:

$$Q^{\pi}(s_t, a_t) = \mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[r_t + \gamma V^{\pi}(s_{t+1})\right]$$

where the expectation is taken over the reward $r_ t$ and the next state $s_ {t+1}$ drawn from the environment given $(s_ t, a_ t)$. Hence, conditioning on $(s_ t, a_ t)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[\delta_t^{V^\pi}\right]
&= \mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[r_t + \gamma V^{\pi}(s_{t+1}) - V^{\pi}(s_t)\right] \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{r_t,\, s_{t+1} \,\mid\, s_t, a_t}\left[r_t + \gamma V^{\pi}(s_{t+1})\right] - V^{\pi}(s_t) \\
&= Q^{\pi}(s_t, a_t) - V^{\pi}(s_t) = A^{\pi}(s_t, a_t).
\end{aligned}
$$

In this ideal situation, we would be equipped with an unbiased low-variance estimator of $A^{\pi}(s_ t,a_ t)$. Unfortunately, we often have some bias in our estimate $V$ of $V^\pi$. Yet, what would happen if we "looked more steps into the future" to estimate our advantage? Let's define:

$$\hat{A}_t^{(k)} := r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \cdots + \gamma^{k-1} r_{t+k-1} + \gamma^k V(s_{t+k}) -V(s_t)$$

For all $k$, $\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$ is an estimate of the advantage whose reliance on $V$ to estimate the return decreases with $k$ as $\gamma^k V(s_ {t+k}) \rightarrow 0$. The bigger $k$ is, the less-biased our estimate of the return is, at the cost of an increased variance.

To see why, it helps to split $\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$ into a sampled part and a bootstrapped part:

$$\hat{A}_t^{(k)} = \underbrace{\Big(r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \cdots + \gamma^{k-1}r_{t+k-1}\Big)}_{\text{sum of } k \text{ sampled rewards}} \; + \; \underbrace{\gamma^k V(s_{t+k})}_{\text{bootstrap}} \; - \; V(s_t)$$

Each sampled reward we add to the first term injects a fresh piece of randomness coming from the environment's transition and reward distributions, so the variance of this part tends to grow as $k$ increases. The bootstrap term, on the other hand, replaces an entire random tail of future rewards with an expectation of that tail $V(s_ {t+k})$ which has much lower variance, but it is only correct if $V = V^\pi$, which is exactly where the bias comes from.

Let's note that, using a telescoping sum (for instance $\hat{A}_ t^{(2)} = (r_ t + \gamma V(s_ {t+1}) - V(s_ t)) + \gamma (r_ {t+1} + \gamma V(s_ {t+2}) - V(s_ {t+1}))$), this estimator can be rewritten as:

$$\hat{A}_t^{(k)} =  \sum_{l=0}^{k-1} \gamma^l \delta_{t+l}^V$$

This will be useful to obtain a practical expression of our final estimator later.

Yet one question remains, how to select $k$? This question is difficult to answer, and rather than making a choice, the authors of the paper propose to use an exponentially-weighted average of these estimators, introducing a parameter $\lambda \in (0,1)$<sup><a href="#note-2" id="ref-2">[2]</a></sup>.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\hat{A}_t^{\text{GAE}(\gamma,\lambda)} 
&:= (1-\lambda)\left(\hat{A}_t^{(1)} + \lambda\hat{A}_t^{(2)} + \lambda^2\hat{A}_t^{(3)} + \ldots\right) \\
&= (1-\lambda)(\delta_t^V + \lambda(\delta_t^V + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V) + \lambda^2(\delta_t^V + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V + \gamma^2\delta_{t+2}^V) + \ldots) \\
&= (1-\lambda)(\delta_t^V(1 + \lambda + \lambda^2 + \ldots) + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V(\lambda + \lambda^2 + \lambda^3 + \ldots) \\
&\quad + \gamma^2\delta_{t+2}^V(\lambda^2 + \lambda^3 + \lambda^4 + \ldots) + \ldots) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\left(\delta_t^V\left(\frac{1}{1-\lambda}\right) + \gamma\delta_{t+1}^V\left(\frac{\lambda}{1-\lambda}\right) + \gamma^2\delta_{t+2}^V\left(\frac{\lambda^2}{1-\lambda}\right) + \ldots\right) \\
&= \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V
\end{aligned}
$$

This last line is what makes the computation easier in practice.

To build some intuition about this estimator, let's look at its expression for the two limit values of $\lambda$:

- In the limit $\lambda \rightarrow 1$, we get that:

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,1)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \gamma^l \delta_{t+l}^V = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \gamma^l r_{t+l} - V(s_t)$$

where the last equality follows from the same telescoping argument as before.

Thus our estimate of the advantage involves a Monte-Carlo estimate of the return! This reduces bias, but makes it very noisy.

- On the other hand, in the limit $\lambda \rightarrow 0$, we get that:

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,0)} = \delta_t^V = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$$

Thus, our estimate of the advantage only uses the 1-step TD-error. This reduces variance a lot, but we rely on an inexact value function $V$ which can introduce bias.

These two limit cases give us intuition about why this exponential averaging of the $\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$ family was introduced: it enables us to smoothly interpolate the bias-variance tradeoff by varying $\lambda$.

## How is GAE implemented in practice?

### On the PPO Loss

Now that we have constructed our estimator of the Advantage function. Let's see how it can be used in practice. For this, we will focus on one of the most popular policy-based method in RL literature: PPO.

Let's recall that the loss at timestep $t$ in PPO is written as:

$$
L_t(\theta) = L_t^{CLIP}(\theta) - c_1 L_t^{VF}(\theta) + c_2 S[\pi_\theta],
$$

Here, $S[\pi_ \theta]$ is an entropy bonus that encourages exploration by discouraging an overly deterministic policy. Since it's not related to our advantage estimate $\hat{A}_ t$, we will set it aside and focus on the clipped surrogate objective $L_ t^{CLIP}$ and the value-function loss $L_ t^{VF}$.

We have:

$$
L_t^{CLIP}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\left[\min(r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t)\right]
$$

And:

$$
L_t^{VF}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\left[(\hat{V}_t - V_{\theta}(s_t))^2\right]
$$

In these two losses, it seems that two estimators are needed, $\hat{A}_ t$ and $\hat{V}_ t$. To see where $\hat{V}_ t$ comes from, it helps to go back to the $k$-step formulation of GAE: recalling that $\hat{A}_ t^{(k)} = \sum_ {l=0}^{k-1}\gamma^l r_ {t+l} + \gamma^k V_ {\theta_ {old}}(s_ {t+k}) - V_ {\theta_ {old}}(s_ t)$ and $\hat{A}_ t^{GAE(\gamma,\lambda)} = (1-\lambda)\sum_ {k=1}^{\infty}\lambda^{k-1}\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$, we can write:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\hat{V}_t := \hat{A}_t^{GAE(\gamma,\lambda)} + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t)
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} \hat{A}_t^{(k)} + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} \Big[\underbrace{\sum_{l=0}^{k-1}\gamma^l r_{t+l} + \gamma^k V_{\theta_{old}}(s_{t+k})}_{=: \, G_t^{(k)}} \;-\; V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t)\Big] + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} G_t^{(k)} \;-\; \underbrace{(1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\lambda^{k-1}}_{=\,1} V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) + V_{\theta_{old}}(s_t) \\
&= (1-\lambda)\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{k-1} G_t^{(k)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Here $G_ t^{(k)} = \sum_ {l=0}^{k-1}\gamma^l r_ {t+l} + \gamma^k V_ {\theta_ {old}}(s_ {t+k})$ is exactly the bootstrapped $k$-step return, so $\hat{V}_ t$ is an exponentially-weighted average of these returns — precisely the $\lambda$-return used as the regression target in $TD(\lambda)$. This derivation also reveals where the $+\,V_ {\theta_ {old}}(s_ t)$ term comes from: because the mixing weights $(1-\lambda)\lambda^{k-1}$ sum to one, adding it exactly cancels the baseline $-\,V_ {\theta_ {old}}(s_ t)$ present in every $\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$, turning our weighted average of *advantages* back into a weighted average of *returns*. Yet, it's a good thing to see that the GAE computation directly hands us the regression target used in $L_ t^{VF}$, if we also keep in memory the value of that state.

That said, this identification is more of an inspiration than an exact equivalence. The classical $TD(\lambda)$ target estimates $V^{\pi}$ from trajectories collected under $\pi$ itself, whereas in PPO the rollouts used to build $\hat{A}_ t$ — and hence $\hat{V}_ t$ — are collected under the old policy $\pi_ {\theta_ {old}}$, while $V_ \theta$ is being fit to approximate $V^{\pi_ \theta}$, the value function of the policy currently being optimized. This target $\hat{V}_ t$ can therefore be understood as a practical, off-policy approximation inspired by the $TD(\lambda)$ idea, rather than an exact computation of it.

### Truncating the sum

Now that we've constructed this nice interpolating estimator, how to use it in practice in a manner that optimizes the hardware we have?

Without getting into too much detail, it's useful to have in mind that our RL training setup often involves a Learner, which learns the policy and value function (often a GPU), and parallel Actors, GPUs or CPUs who generate trajectories the Learner uses to improve itself.

How is the GAE estimate computed efficiently in this setup? Using an infinite sum doesn't seem to be the best way to learn efficiently. Indeed, we will need to produce full trajectories which can be long, or even impossible. In this time, our Learner (a GPU) will sit idle, which we want to avoid at all cost.

To solve this issue, for each actor, we collect fixed-length segments of $T$ transitions $(s_ t,\, a_ t,\, r_ t,\, s_ {t+1},\, V_ {\theta_ \text{old}}(s_ t))_ {t=0}^{T-1}$, which will enable to construct batches we'll feed to the GPU for training. The stored $V_ {\theta_ \text{old}}(s_ t)$ is needed to compute each $\delta_ t^V = r_ t + \gamma V_ {\theta_ \text{old}}(s_ {t+1}) - V_ {\theta_ \text{old}}(s_ t)$ later. For a transition at time $t$ of this mini-trajectory, we can compute the truncated advantage:

$$\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{T-t-1} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V$$

To see where this upper limit comes from, let's write out the truncated estimates for a segment of length $T$ explicitly:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\hat{A}_{T-1} &= \delta_{T-1}^V \\
\hat{A}_{T-2} &= \delta_{T-2}^V + \gamma\lambda\,\delta_{T-1}^V \\
\hat{A}_{T-3} &= \delta_{T-3}^V + \gamma\lambda\,\delta_{T-2}^V + (\gamma\lambda)^2\delta_{T-1}^V \\
              &\vdots \\
\hat{A}_t     &= \sum_{l=0}^{T-1-t}(\gamma\lambda)^l\,\delta_{t+l}^V
\end{aligned}
$$

Each row is the previous one multiplied by $\gamma\lambda$ and prepended with $\delta_ t^V$, which gives the backward recurrence $\hat{A}_ t = \delta_ t^V + \gamma\lambda\,\hat{A}_ {t+1}$. In practice this is exactly how the computation is done: initialise $\hat{A}_ {T-1} = \delta_ {T-1}^V$ and sweep backwards through the segment in a single pass. This is both elegant and very efficient!

The choice of $T$ varies according to the setup of the problem and system configuration (bigger $T$ often meaning bigger batch size). Yet, intuitively, we need to ensure that the truncated sum carries enough information about the future. The notion of effective horizon helps us make this choice properly.

### GAE effective horizon

Let's recall that the untruncated advantage estimator is given by the infinite sum:

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V$$

The weights $(\gamma\lambda)^l$ applied to the TD-errors shrink geometrically with $l$. The key question we need to answer is: after how many steps does the term $(\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_ {t+l}^V$ become negligible?

The natural way to measure this is to read the geometric decay as an exponential one. Writing $(\gamma\lambda)^l = e^{l\ln(\gamma\lambda)} = e^{-l/\tau}$, the weights decay with a time constant

$$\tau = -\frac{1}{\ln(\gamma\lambda)} \approx \frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda},$$

where the approximation uses the first-order Taylor expansion $\ln(\gamma\lambda) \approx \gamma\lambda - 1$ around $1$. By analogy with any exponential decay, after $\tau$ steps the weight has fallen to a fraction $e^{-1}$ of its initial value. We therefore call

$$H = \frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda}$$

the effective horizon: it is the characteristic number of steps over which the TD-errors still carry appreciable weight.<sup><a href="#note-4" id="ref-4">[4]</a></sup>

It is worth keeping in mind that $H$ is only the $1/e$ scale, not a point beyond which the tail vanishes. The fraction of the total weight captured by the first $H$ terms is

$$\frac{\sum_{l=0}^{H-1}(\gamma\lambda)^l}{\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(\gamma\lambda)^l} = 1 - (\gamma\lambda)^{H} \approx 1 - e^{-1} \approx 63\%.$$

So truncating exactly at $T = H$ still discards roughly a third of the weight. If we want to retain a fraction $1-\epsilon$ of it, we need $(\gamma\lambda)^T \leq \epsilon$, i.e. $T \gtrsim \ln(1/\epsilon)\,H$; for instance capturing $95\%$ of the weight requires $T \gtrsim 3H$. In practice $H$ is the minimum sensible scale for $T$, and a small multiple of it is the safe choice.

What goes wrong if $T$ is chosen too small? The truncated estimator differs from the exact one by the discarded tail, and this error is controlled precisely by $(\gamma\lambda)^T$:

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\lambda)} - \hat{A}_t^{(T)} = \sum_{l=T-t}^{\infty}(\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^V, \qquad \Big|\hat{A}_t^{GAE(\lambda)} - \hat{A}_t^{(T)}\Big| \leq \frac{(\gamma\lambda)^{T-t}}{1-\gamma\lambda}\,\max_{l}\big|\delta_{l}^V\big|.$$

The table below makes this concrete: for a fixed segment length $T$, the residual weight $(\gamma\lambda)^T$ is what is left on the floor. As soon as $T$ is comparable to a few times $H$, this factor is small and the truncation is harmless; when $T$ is of the order of $H$ or below, a non-negligible share of the signal is lost.

| $\gamma$ | $\lambda$ | $H = \frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda}$ | $T$ | $(\gamma\lambda)^{T}$ |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 0.99  | 0.95 | 16.81 | 3  | 0.83 |
| 0.997 | 0.95 | 18.92 | 10 | 0.58 |
| 0.997 | 0.80 | 4.94  | 10 | 0.10 |
| 0.997 | 0.90 | 9.73  | 20 | 0.11 |

*Effective horizon $H = \frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda}$ and residual truncation weight $(\gamma\lambda)^T$ for various $\gamma,\lambda,T$. The first two rows use $T \lesssim H$ and leave a large fraction of the weight in the tail; the last two use $T \approx 2H$ and bring the residual down to roughly $10\%$.*

## Different practical choices

But now that we've finished studying the theoretical and practical whereabouts of GAE, one question remains: how do we decide the value of all these parameters : $\lambda, \gamma, T$? The answer is: well... it depends on the problem settings.

Nevertheless, there are two interesting use cases to mention to build intuition:

- For a long time, the standard value of $\lambda$ has been $0.95$. This worked well in practice in the original article and in most "classical" RL settings (especially robotics, MuJoCo…). Indeed, in these types of problems, we often benefit from high-speed actors running in parallel with dense rewards, which enable us to set $T$ at high values compared to the effective horizon $H$. In this regime, truncation is essentially harmless and $\lambda$ acts as the primary dial for the bias-variance tradeoff.

- In other settings, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)<sup><a href="#note-5" id="ref-5">[5]</a></sup>, the convention is to use $\gamma=1$, as the reward signal arrives only at the end of the sequence and discounting would otherwise penalise longer responses. $\lambda$ remains a tunable parameter. Setting $\lambda=1$ would give a Monte-Carlo estimate of the return, but the increased variance can hamper training. Recent research<sup><a href="#note-6" id="ref-6">[6]</a></sup> argues that $\lambda$ has a significant impact on PPO performance for mathematical reasoning, proposing to decouple $\lambda$ for the advantage estimate from $\lambda$ for the value target, and to scale the former proportionally to the token sequence length.

## Conclusion 

Overall, GAE offers a very useful way of estimating the advantage function. By offering a spectrum of estimators (with the choice of $\lambda$) it enables us to finely choose how we want to position on the bias-variance tradeoff. As we've seen, the common choice $\lambda = 0.95$ seems prevalent in the literature. Nevertheless, we should bear in mind that the equilibrium between $\lambda, \gamma, T$ and other problem settings is at the heart of making this technique work in practice.

The key is that $\hat{A}_ t^{GAE(\gamma,\lambda)}$ is a convex combination $\sum_ {k\geq 1}(1-\lambda)\lambda^{k-1}\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$ of the individual estimators, and both bias and variance respond predictably to such combinations. By linearity of expectation, the bias of this weighted sum is exactly the same weighted average of the biases of the $\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$, so it is bounded between the largest bias (achieved at $k=1$, i.e. $\lambda \to 0$, where the estimate leans most heavily on the possibly-inaccurate $V$) and the smallest one (achieved as $k \to \infty$, i.e. $\lambda \to 1$, where the estimate becomes an unbiased Monte-Carlo return); pushing $\lambda$ towards $0$ or $1$ shifts the weight towards one end of this range. Variance behaves similarly in spirit, although less cleanly because the $\hat{A}_ t^{(k)}$ are correlated (they share most of their terms): increasing $\lambda$ shifts weight towards estimators that rely more on sampled rewards and less on bootstrapping with $V$, which is precisely what drives variance up. This is why $\lambda$ acts as a smooth dial between the two extremes we derived above, rather than an arbitrary hyperparameter.
